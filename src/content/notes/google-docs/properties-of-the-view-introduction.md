**A monist account of moral realism.
**In this piece, I will describe and argue for a certain kind of moral realism, one which I have not seen described elsewhere: a cognitivist, judgement naturalist, metaphysical non-naturalist, moral realism [-1]. This view rejects the traditional natural/supernatural distinction and proposes the idea of epistemic realms to explain the relation between phenomenological and physical. Before I fully describe this view and argue for it, I’d like to simply list its key properties. In this view…
There are objective normative facts. 
Normativity stems directly from the way reality is.
Value properties are part of how the world is, not an extra “projection” we add on top of a value-free reality.
Morality is non-arbitrary. 
Sufficient understanding of reality allows one to see what’s good.
… and in a manner that makes the good actually seem good. [1][2]
We have epistemic access to (some) normative facts via consciousness and valence.[2]
Normative stuff is infused into the “natural” world via consciousness and valence.
Reason alone is *not *enough to understand normative facts.
Normative stuff has a causal interaction with the “natural” world. [3]
Moral facts – the objective moral truth – casually affect motivations and behavior in some minds.
This view…
Makes predictions about AI systems, superintelligences, and alien intelligences.
Expects moral convergence as intelligence increases *if* consciousness and valence are present in the mind.
Rejects orthogonality thesis
Regarding metaphysics/ontology, this view says:
The traditional natural/supernatural distinction leads to confusion with regards to consciousness/phenomenology and normativity.
The natural/supernatural should be replaced by thinking about different *epistemic realms.*
The physical domain and the phenomenological domain differ with regards to their epistemic properties / access / closure.
Phenomenological facts (facts about consciousness) belong to a different epistemic realm than physical facts.
Normative facts belong to a different epistemic realm than physical facts.
None of this implies dualism. In fact, the most natural metaphysics for this view is neutral monism. [4][5]
In this neutral monism, physical and phenomenological are aspects of a single underlying substance. Neither aspect is privileged.
