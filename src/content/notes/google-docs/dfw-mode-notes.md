It's a recognition that **certain truths can only be conveyed through a kind of performative enactment**, that showing and telling aren't really separable activities when what you're trying to show/tell is something about the nature of showing and telling itself.

And this is where the real paradox emerges, because Wallace seems to want both things at once:** he wants language to be transparent, a medium for genuine human connection and communication, but he also wants it to be opaque, strange, defamiliarized** in exactly the way Shklovsky and Gass describe. He wants to create **a voice that feels like it could be anyone's voice, but he also wants that voice to do things that no ordinary voice could do**. He wants to be democratic and accessible, but he also wants to be an Artist with a capital A, doing the kind of consciousness-altering work that only Artists can do.

The medium really is the message, as McLuhan said (and as Gass demonstrates), but not in some simple deterministic way. Rather, the medium is the message because meaning isn't something that exists independently of its articulation, waiting to be clothed in words like a body in garments. Meaning emerges in and through the act of articulation itself, which means that **any attempt to talk about meaning-making necessarily becomes an instance of meaning-making**. **This is why Wallace's grocery store scene works the way it does: not because it illustrates a philosophical point about consciousness and attention (though it does that too), but because it creates an experience of heightened consciousness and attention in the reader. It doesn't just tell us that we should see the sacred in the mundane, it actually makes us see the sacred in the mundane, if only for the duration of the reading experience.** And this is why Shklovsky has to quote Tolstoy at such length, why Gass has to make his prose musical, why Wallace has to turn his commencement address into a work of art.

It's a reminder that the distinction between art and life, between theory and practice, between criticism and creation, is itself a kind of automatization, a habitual way of thinking that prevents us from seeing what's really there.
