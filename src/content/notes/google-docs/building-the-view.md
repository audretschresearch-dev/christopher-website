**Comparison to Taoism
**C.S. Lewis described morality as “the *Tao *– some kind of natural law; a way that rightly reflects and responds to the world; an ethics that is objective, authoritative, and deeply tied to the nature of Being itself.” Taoism does not speak in the language of western philosophy. Still (and with full acknowledgement that this is probably not Tao-approved), we can consider it in these terms. And we shall see that it provides an interesting account of things that is actually quite similar to mine.
In Taoism, there is a right way and a wrong way to act/be. This moral “law” exists and applies to you, regardless of whether or not you think it does. This law comes directly from some kind of fundamental reality, the Tao. By understanding the Tao, one both understands how to act and is motivated to act in that way. How can we come to understand the Tao? Not through reason or language – “the Tao that can be named is not the Tao.” The Tao is ineffable. It can be grasped only through some kind of direct experience itself. 
Now the western philosophers will want to ask if the Tao is natural. If the Tao is natural, they’ll say, then it can only impart facts about the natural world; it will not be able to cross the is-ought gap. If the Tao is supernatural, then it won’t be able to causally affect the natural world. Taoism replies: mu. This is a false dichotomy. There is no natural vs supernatural. There is no is vs ought. There is only one thing, the Tao, and you are confused because it has two aspects. 

It’s accurate to call this view a form of moral realism. With regards to moral motivation it’s internalist. With regards to naturalism it’s… neither.
