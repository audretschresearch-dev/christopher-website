**Title
**By Christopher Audretsch

**Introduction
**In this piece, I’ll present a new conception of moral realism. This view is deeply appealing along multiple axes. It offers an account of morality *the way I want it to be: *morality is real, objective, fully grounded, somewhat epistemically accessible, and non-arbitrary. It addresses the is-ought problem. It sheds some light on the mind-body problem. This is a view in which the physical, the phenomenological, and the moral are entangled and deeply related. Each is part of reality and affects the others but is not reducible to another. This is a view in which we can accept consciousness as being something special, real, and ineffable – that is, the way it appears to us. 
But how can such a view be possible? By recognizing how the naturalism/non-naturalism dichotomy leads us astray. Views on moral realism are usually considered to be either naturalist or non-naturalist; this view is technically non-naturalist, but it finds this framing to be obscuring. The key is to recognize that reality contains distinct epistemic realms. The physical and the phenomenological belong to distinct epistemic realms, and science only has proper epistemic access to the physical realm. These realms are causally connected, which only seems problematic because the natural/non-natural distinction tends to conflate being casually connected with being reducible. 
**1
**Let me start by describing my view very quickly. 
The view has** **features that look naturalist**:
**Normative facts are grounded in reality; they’re simply part of how the world is
They're causally entangled with the physical world 
They're not arbitrary or written in an envelope
(Some) minds have epistemic access to these facts
The view has features that look non-naturalist**:
**Normative properties are irreducibly different from paradigmatic natural properties (mass, charge, molecular structure)
They exist in a separate epistemic realm requiring special access
You cannot derive normative truths from third-person physical observation alone
They are, in an important sense, "over and above" what's accessible through science
**2
***A preliminary note about this section: The point of this section is to contextualize the view I’m proposing, not to discuss naturalism/non-naturalism in depth. I don’t want to argue about terminology – it’s easy to get stuck here. So I’ll be going very fast. 
*First things first: this view is a kind of moral realism. Moral realism says that there are objective normative facts that hold independently of what anyone thinks about them. 
The traditional next question is then whether normative facts are ‘natural’ or ‘non-natural’, where ‘natural’ means something like “that which can be studied by science”. Moral naturalism then the view that “moral facts are the kinds of facts that can be investigated by (broadly) scientific methods”, and moral non-naturalism is the view that moral facts are non-natural. As philosophers spend more time thinking about these views and exploring their implications, their conception of natural and non-natural expands. In general, the debate ends up dividing things like so: 
**Natural facts**: Part of the causal nexus, accessible to science, grounded in the physical world, reducible to physical properties
**Non-natural facts**: Floating above the physical, causally inert, requiring mysterious intuition, irreducibly separate
This division seems to force moral realists into an uncomfortable choice:
**Naturalism: **Then normative properties seem just like any other natural property (charge, mass), and the is-ought gap remains unbridged. So what are we really talking about here?
**Non-naturalism:** Then normative facts float mysteriously above the causal order, with no clear epistemic access. And if normativity isn't grounded in the natural world, it's unclear where it comes from or why it has the specific content it does. 
*For further elucidation on the problems with these views, see Carlsmith's pieces on** *__*non-naturalist realism*__* and** *__*meta-ethical hedonism*__*. 
***3
**So, that’s the extremely condensed version of naturalism/non-naturalism. Now I can tell you how we got pointed in the wrong direction and why the above division is wrong. We conflated the ontological with the epistemic; that is, we didn’t have a way to distinguish “things that exist (in the sense of having causal power in the physical world)” and “things that science can study”.
Consider consciousness. Is consciousness natural or non-natural? 
It's clearly part of reality, not some floating addition
Yet science (third-person observation) cannot determine if consciousness exists
Or if they can, they don’t really understand what consciousness is
Only conscious beings have epistemic access through first-person acquaintance
Non-conscious entities would see a complete physical description with consciousness as an "unnecessary" addition
Consciousness isn't floating above reality nor is it reducible to natural properties like mass or charge. It belongs to a different** ***epistemic realm.
***4
**Different aspects of reality require different modes of epistemic access. These differing modes of access lead to different sets of facts. (One could also use the term “knowledge” or “information” or “things” instead of “facts"). These form distinct epistemic realms, of which there are (at least) three:
**Realm 1: The Scientific 
**Realm 1 consists of the things that can be studied and understood by science. Why don’t I call this realm The Natural, given that this is the very definition of “natural”? Because I want to avoid the preexisting ideas and associations we have with "natural", particularly its conceptual association with things like “real”. Realm 1 is accessible through third-person observation and measurement. Examples of facts in this realm are: the speed of light, mass of particular objects, behavior of weather, and properties of plants. 
**Realm 2: The Phenomenological
**Realm 2 consists of things that can only be known to minds with consciousness. The canonical fact in this realm is the existence of consciousness/qualia. I’m not going to belabor this point; much ink has been spilled over this idea, and you probably already know whether you agree with it or not. I will point out that this realm has a certain ineffability that Realm 1 does not. Other things in this realm, like things about particular qualia e.g. what it’s like to taste a lemon, cannot exactly be formulated as “facts”. (See my double usage of “things” in that sentence). Yet they are parts of reality all the same.
**Realm 3: The Valenced
**Realm 3 consists of things that can only be known to conscious minds that experience positive and negative valence. Canonical examples here are the goodness of pleasure and the badness of suffering, or that experiencing pleasure is better than experiencing suffering *ceteris paribus* (all else being equal). Hey, that’s a normative fact! 
If you’re having trouble distinguishing this from Realm 2, imagine a life in which every experience feels neutral, or in which one is neutral for some extended time interval. Some genres of depression seem to be like this: one feels totally “flat”.
**Possible Realm 4+**: 
There may be “higher” epistemic realms accessible only to superintelligent minds ("metaconsciousness"). These may contain deeper normative truths that humans can't access. (We will come back to this idea later.)
**4
**Earlier, I said that consciousness is neither supernatural (floating above reality) nor is it reducible to Realm 1 natural properties (like mass or charge). It’s best understood as belonging to a separate epistemic realm. So too with normativity, I claim.
**Normative facts are grounded in reality** (the "naturalist" feature): They don't float above the world as arbitrary rules. They're intrinsically woven into reality through valence experiences (and possibly in other ways). When you experience suffering, that experience *is* something that matters. And the fact that it matters doesn’t come from there being a rule somewhere that says that suffering matters, or from God ordaining this to be the case. That experience matters by virtue of what it is. The normative property is right there in the phenomenology, not added afterwards.
**Normative facts are irreducible and require special access** (the "non-naturalist" feature): They belong to Realm 3 (valence/normativity), which is epistemically distinct from Realm 1 (physical properties accessible to third-person science). You cannot derive 'suffering is bad' from any complete description of the universe in purely Realm 1 terms (physics, chemistry, neuroscience), just as you cannot derive 'consciousness exists' from a purely physical description. A being without valence experience cannot access normative facts, just as a non-conscious being cannot access facts about consciousness.
This view explains several puzzles at once.
**Against "floating" non-naturalism**: Normative facts aren't causally inert entities mysteriously floating above reality. They're grounded in valence, infused into the natural world via consciousness, and affect behavior through the motivations of valent beings.
**Against reductive naturalism**: You can't just identify "goodness" with some Realm 1 property and be done. Normative facts are irreducible—direct acquaintance with certain phenomenal states (strong valence) is required for normative knowledge.
The epistemic realms framework shows that we can have robust realism (objective facts grounded in reality, causally entangled with the world) without reduction or mystery (without either collapsing normativity to physics or positing floating platonic entities). The apparent dilemma between naturalism and non-naturalism dissolves once we stop conflating ontological and epistemic questions
