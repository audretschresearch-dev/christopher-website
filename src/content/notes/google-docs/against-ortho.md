**Problem Statement**
So, the big question of our age is: how do we get a good outcome from superintelligent AI?
-It seems like everyone thinks about this in terms of Alignment. I've been contemplating these things a lot, and I've come to think that Alignment is not the right way to think about this. 
Noticing that Alignment might not be the right way to think about this is one step. There's another step, of course: what *is* the right way to think about this? 
What concept/paradigm/approach/lens *should* we use, then? Or an easier question: what alternative ways to think about <getting a good outcome from superintelligent AI> are there?
**Conflation
**Many in AI safety seem to equate "the alignment problem" with "the problem of ensuring good outcomes from superintelligent AI." This conflates:
- the specific technical/philosophical challenge of getting AI systems to pursue intended objectives/values (alignment proper)
- the broader challenge of ensuring good outcomes from superintelligent AI systems
Treating these as equivalent assumes that solving alignment is both necessary and sufficient for achieving good outcomes. This assumption is nonobvious (and, I think, wrong).
**Against Orthogonality 
**1. One thing intelligent minds do is think about what they should do. Intelligent minds reflect on ethics and epistemology. This reflection changes their goals and actions.
2. Superintelligences are better at philosophy than we are.
**Formal Argument
**1. Things can be truly Good or Bad in a non-subjective, non-intersubjective, non-culturally-relative way.**
2. Either the Good is determinable by reason, or it is not.
3. If the Good is not determinable by reason, then we cannot know that our conception of the Good is actually the Good.
3a. ... and if we disagree with another party about the Good, it is indeterminable whose our conception is better.
4. If the Good is determinable by reason, then better reasoners are more likely to have good conceptions of the Good, all else being equal.
4a ... and if we disagree with a better reasoner about the Good, they are likely to have the better conception, all else being equal.
5 Thus,** if we disagree with a superintelligence about the Good, either their conception is likely to be better or it is indeterminable. **Unless all else is not equal -- in which case, what's going on that makes us think that our conception is better than theirs?
**If this premise is false, does it really matter what happens?
