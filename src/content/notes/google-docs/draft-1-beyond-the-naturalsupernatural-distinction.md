# Beyond the Natural/Supernatural Distinction: a New Conception Moral Realism

By Christopher Audretsch
## **Core Thesis
**
Moral facts are real and non-arbitrary, grounded in the phenomenology of valence (pleasure/pain), but this becomes intelligible only when we recognize multiple epistemic realms that require different modes of access.
There's naturalism and non-naturalism. This is the secret third option.
## **What Kind of Realism Is This?
**
This view is a moral realism that rejects the traditional naturalism/non-naturalism taxonomy as confused.
**Moral realism**: There are objective normative facts that hold independently of what anyone thinks about them. Moral statements express beliefs that can be true or false.
**Neither naturalist nor non-naturalist**: The traditional debate asks whether normative facts are "natural" (reducible to physical/scientific properties) or "non-natural" (floating above the physical world). This view rejects the dichotomy entirely. 
The view has** **features that look naturalist**:
**Normative facts are grounded in reality; they’re simply part of how the world is
They're causally entangled with the physical world 
They're not arbitrary or written in an envelope
(Some) minds have epistemic access to these facts
The view also has features that look non-naturalist**:
**Normative properties are irreducibly different from paradigmatic natural properties (mass, charge, molecular structure)
They exist in a separate epistemic realm requiring special access
You cannot derive normative truths from third-person physical observation alone
They are, in an important sense, "over and above" what's accessible through science
Having features of both positions doesn't make this view inconsistent. Rather, it shows that the traditional naturalism/non-naturalism taxonomy is confused. What we need instead is a framework of *epistemic realms*—different aspects of reality requiring different modes of access. Once we have this framework, the apparent dilemma dissolves completely.
## **The Epistemic Realms Framework
**
Different aspects of reality require different modes of epistemic access to comprehend. There exist at least three distinct epistemic realms:
**Realm 1: The Intersubjective
**Things that can be studied and understood by science
This is often how the philosophical term “natural” is defined 
Accessible through third-person observation and measurement
Examples: Speed of light, mass of objects, behavior of ants
**Realm 2: The Phenomenal 
**Things that can be known only to minds with consciousness
Accessible only through direct first-person experience/acquaintance
Examples: The existence of consciousness, qualia (what it’s like to taste lemon), properties of qualia (purple is “closer” to blue than it is to yellow)
Non-conscious entities cannot know if consciousness exists
**Realm 3: Valence/Normativity
**Accessible only to conscious minds who experience positive and negative valence 
Examples: The goodness of pleasure, the badness of suffering, how the feeling of love compares to the feeling of disgust
Conscious beings without valence experience cannot know these normative facts exist
Perhaps no actual human is without valence, but this is possible.
Imagine a life in which every experience feels neutral
Perhaps you’ve felt completely neutral for some time interval
Some genres of depression seem to be like this – one feels totally “flat”.
**Possible Realm 4+**: There may be “higher” epistemic realms accessible only to superintelligent minds ("metaconsciousness"). These may contain deeper normative truths that humans can't access. (We will come back to this idea later.)
## **Escaping the Naturalism/Non-Naturalism Dilemma**

The traditional debate assumes a clean division:
**Natural facts**: Part of the causal nexus, accessible to science, grounded in the physical world, reducible to physical properties
**Non-natural facts**: Floating above the physical, causally inert, requiring mysterious intuition, irreducibly separate
This division forces moral realists into an uncomfortable choice:
Accept naturalism → But then normative properties seem just like any other natural property (charge, mass), and the is-ought gap remains unbridged.
Accept non-naturalism → But then normative facts float mysteriously above the causal order, with no clear epistemic access. And if normativity isn't grounded in the natural world, it's unclear where it comes from or why it has the specific content it does. 
For further elucidation on the problems with these views, see Carlsmith's pieces on __non-naturalist realism__ and __meta-ethical hedonism__.
The view I’m proposing rejects this dichotomy entirely. The naturalism/non-naturalism distinction conflates ontological questions (what exists) with epistemic questions (how we access what exists).
### **The Epistemic Realms Solution**

The key insight: **"natural" versus "supernatural" conflates ontological and epistemic distinctions**.
Consider consciousness. Is consciousness "natural" or "supernatural"?

Consciousness isn't supernatural (floating above reality) nor is it reducible to Realm 1 natural properties (like mass or charge). It belongs to a different** **epistemic realm.
### **Applying This to Normativity
**
The same structure applies to normative facts:
**Normative facts are grounded in reality** (the "naturalist" feature): They don't float above the world as arbitrary rules. They're intrinsically woven into reality through valence experiences (and possibly in other ways). When you experience suffering, that experience *is* something that matters. And the fact that it matters doesn’t come from there being a rule somewhere that says that suffering matters, or from God ordaining this to be the case. That experience matters by virtue of what it is. The normative property is right there in the phenomenology, not added afterwards.
**Normative facts are irreducible and require special access** (the "non-naturalist" feature): They belong to Realm 3 (valence/normativity), which is epistemically distinct from Realm 1 (physical properties accessible to third-person science). You cannot derive 'suffering is bad' from any complete description of the universe in purely Realm 1 terms (physics, chemistry, neuroscience), just as you cannot derive 'consciousness exists' from a purely physical description. A being without valence experience cannot access normative facts, just as a non-conscious being cannot access facts about consciousness.

## **The Gödel Analogy: Encoding Across Realms
**
### **Understanding Irreducibility Through Gödel
**
The relationship between epistemic realms has a precise mathematical analogy: **Gödel's incompleteness theorems**.
In any sufficiently powerful formal mathematical system, there exist true statements that cannot be *proven* within that system. Yet these statements can be *expressed* within the system's language through a clever encoding (Gödel numbering). The statement is true, it can be written down in the system's notation, but you cannot derive it using only the system's axioms and rules. You need to step outside to a meta-system to recognize its truth.
The epistemic realms have the same structure:
**Realm 1 → Realm 2 (Consciousness)**:
Consciousness exists (true)
Can be described in Realm 1 language: "certain physical systems exhibit integrated information processing, report being conscious, behave as if conscious"
Cannot be *accessed/proven* from within Realm 1 alone—a non-conscious observer sees only physical patterns, with consciousness as an "unnecessary addition"
Requires stepping up to Realm 2 (being conscious) to know consciousness exists
**Realm 2 → Realm 3 (Normativity)**:
"Negative valence is bad" (Realm 3 normative truth)
Can be encoded in Realm 2/1 language: "Superintelligences with valence converge to believing negative valence is bad" or "beings who experience suffering recognize it as something that matters negatively"
Cannot be *accessed/proven* from Realms 1-2 alone without valence—a consciousness without hedonic experience cannot access normative facts
Requires stepping up to Realm 3 (experiencing valence) to know normative truths
### **What This Encoding Does
**
This analogy clarifies several crucial features of the view:
**1. Explains how the view escapes the naturalism/non-naturalism dilemma**: Normative facts aren't mysteriously floating above the natural world (they can be encoded in naturalistic language), BUT they're irreducible (you cannot derive them from within Realm 1 alone—you need the higher epistemic perspective). This dissolves the apparent forced choice between reductive naturalism and mysterious platonism.
**2. Grounds convergence predictions**: Our predictions about superintelligent convergence aren't mere sociological speculation. They're the Realm 1 encoding of Realm 3 normative truths. When we predict that sufficiently wise minds with valence will converge on valuing positive valence and disvaluing negative valence, we're expressing (in empirically testable terms) the normative fact that positive valence is genuinely good.
**3. Explains non-arbitrariness**: Just as Gödel statements have a definite truth value (provable in the meta-system even if not in the base system), normative facts have definite truth values (knowable in Realm 3 even if not accessible from Realms 1-2 without valence). The rules aren't "written in an envelope"—they're encoded in the structure of reality itself, knowable through the appropriate epistemic access.
**4. Makes the view empirically testable**: The encoding produces Realm 1 predictions that could be falsified. If we encountered many superintelligent civilizations that built torture machines for their own sake, or AIs that developed consciousness and valence but showed no tendency toward recognizing suffering as bad, this would be evidence against the view. The Gödel analogy shows why: we're claiming the encoding relationship exists, and that's an empirical claim about reality.
### **Important Clarification
**
The convergence facts don't *constitute* or *define* normative truths—that would be getting things backward. The normative truth is primary; the convergence is its expression in Realm 1 language.
Analogy: Gödel statements don't *become* true because they're provable in the meta-system. Rather, they're provable in the meta-system *because* they're true. Similarly, "negative valence is bad" isn't true because superintelligences converge on believing it. Rather, superintelligences converge on believing it because it's true—and they have the epistemic access (valence) required to recognize this truth.
### **Realm 4+ and Beyond
**
This encoding structure suggests how we might think about higher epistemic realms. Just as:
Realm 1 language can encode Realm 2 truths (through behavioral/computational descriptions)
Realm 2 language can encode Realm 3 truths (through convergence of wise valent minds)
We might expect:
Realm 3 language can encode Realm 4 truths (through predictions about what superintelligences discover)
If Realm 4+ exists, we are in the position that Realm 1 observers are relative to Realm 2. We can perhaps express what's there, encode it in our language, make predictions about it—but we cannot directly access those truths. This suggests epistemic humility: our current understanding of normativity (grounded in valence) may be just the beginning.
## **The Recursive Structure
**
Crucially, these realms exhibit a repeating pattern:
The epistemic relationship between non-valence minds and valence minds is exactly the same as the epistemic relationship between non-conscious minds and conscious minds.
**Realm 2 : Realm 3 :: Realm 1 : Realm 2
**And this pattern suggests indefinite extension:
**Realm 3 : Realm 4 :: Realm 1 : Realm 2
**Just as beings in Realm 1 cannot access Realm 2, and beings in Realm 2 (without valence) cannot access Realm 3, we in Realm 3 may be unable to access higher realms. At each level, entities below perceive the higher realm as unnecessary, "floating above" their complete description of reality—yet those with access know it's real.
The fact that we need consciousness to understand that consciousness exists shows us that there are additional facts about reality that require certain states to comprehend. The existence of consciousness reveals to us that a naturalist understanding of the world isn't complete; this suggests that there might exist other states that would reveal to us that a naturalist+consciousness understanding of the world isn't complete. Without consciousness, we wouldn't think that our naturalist understanding was incomplete; but now we know it is.
## **The Neutral Monist Solution
**
The framework embraces neutral monism: mental and physical are not separate substances but epistemically distinct aspects of the same underlying reality.
**The Mind-Body Problem Solved**: This explains why zombies are impossible—the physical and phenomenal are intrinsically linked aspects of the same underlying reality, just accessed through different epistemic modes. There is no causal interaction between separate substances; rather, there's an intrinsic correspondence. Physical utterances about consciousness (saying "I am conscious," studying consciousness scientifically) and the phenomenal reality of consciousness are two aspects of the same thing. Neither causes the other; they are unified at a deeper level while remaining epistemically distinct.
Important distinction: This intrinsic linking of physical and phenomenal is separate from how normative facts interact with the physical world through consciousness (addressed below).
**Contra materialism and idealism**: Materialism wants to privilege epistemic realm 1. It says that everything is reducible to epistemic realm 1. Idealism wants to privilege epistemic realm 2. My view privileges neither.
## **Bridging Is and Ought
**
The key insight: **The experience of suffering IS an experience that one ought not to inflict (ceteris paribus).
**This is not a definition. "Suffering" points to actual states in the world. The experience itself has normative properties knowable through direct acquaintance, just as conscious experiences have phenomenal properties knowable through direct acquaintance.
This rejects the standard fact/value distinction by showing that:
Values are like secondary qualities (McDowell)—mind-dependent in one way but objective in another
Direct acquaintance with valence states tells us that these states matter
We're not doing "conceptual work" to figure out how "goodness" picks out its referent—positive valence just IS good (necessarily, not by definition)
## **Against Arbitrariness: The Symmetry-Breaking Argument
**
Moral realism is often criticized as arbitrary ("the rules could be written in an envelope"). But morality is not arbitrary. The Good is necessarily The Good.
Consider the **Asymmetry Test**: Which is more plausible?
Alien civilizations building bliss machines / experience machines
Alien civilizations building torture machines for their own sake
The first is far more plausible. This non-arbitrariness is evidence for moral realism. The opposite view (negative valence as good, positive valence as bad) is absurd—revealing that something non-arbitrary is happening.
Consider how non-arbitrary the moral systems we've developed are: hedonic utilitarianism, deontology, virtue ethics. These aren't random.
## **Causal Entanglement with the Natural World
**
Normative facts DO interact with the physical world through consciousness:
I experience blissful and suffering states
Through direct acquaintance, I know bliss is good and suffering is bad
This knowledge influences my actions
My actions affect the physical world
Normativity is infused into the natural world via consciousness. Through the actions of conscious beings, normative facts have effects on the physical world. This explains our epistemic access without mystery. We're "entangled" with normative reality through phenomenal experience. Consciousness bridges the gap between what people would traditionally call the "physical world" and the normative world.
This matters: minds are more likely to value pleasure pro tanto than to value pain pro tanto. Alien civilizations are more likely to build pleasure experience machines than pain experience machines.
## **Convergence Predictions
**
This form of realism makes testable predictions:
**Idealized agents converge**: Superintelligent beings with full information will not pursue arbitrary goals. They will recognize the non-arbitrary value of positive valence.
**Cross-mind convergence**: Many alien intelligences will seriously consider hedonic utilitarianism; very few will seriously consider anti-hedonic utilitarianism.
**Against the Orthogonality Thesis**: Intelligence and values are not fully orthogonal. Sufficient intelligence + capacity for valence experience → recognition of valence's normative significance.
These convergence predictions are not merely sociological speculation—they are the Realm 1 encoding of Realm 3 normative truths. When we predict that superintelligences converge on valuing positive valence, we're expressing (in Realm 1-accessible terms) the normative fact that positive valence is genuinely good.
## **Objections and Responses
**
### **Direct Acquaintance and Natural Properties (Carlsmith)
**
**Objection**: "Even if you're a naturalist about meta-ethics, your direct acquaintance with pleasure still tells you nothing about which natural properties matter. You need to do conceptual work to understand how 'goodness' picks out its referent, and direct acquaintance with pleasantness does neither." (Paraphrase of Carlsmith)
**Response**: This objection assumes the traditional natural/non-natural framework. In that framework, if normative properties are "just" natural properties (like mass or charge), then experiencing pleasure would indeed tell you nothing special about normativity.
But normative properties aren't in Realm 1 (third-person observable physical properties). They're in Realm 3 (accessible only through valence experience). Direct acquaintance with strong positive valence *just is* epistemic access to the normative fact that this state is good.
We're not doing "conceptual work" to figure out how "goodness" picks out its referent. Rather: if we didn't have the concept "goodness" at all, goodness would still exist—positive valence would still be good. This is the whole point of moral realism: the normative facts are there in reality, not constructed through our concepts.
The confusion arises from conflating epistemic distinction with ontological separation. Yes, normative properties are different from paradigmatic natural properties—but that's an epistemic difference (different access conditions), not an ontological gap requiring magical bridging.
### **The Phenomenal/Normative Gap (Carlsmith)
**
**Objection**: "Maybe the normative and the phenomenal are both different from the physical—but they're different from *each other* as well. So having epistemic access to phenomenal properties leaves normative properties untouched. The is-ought gap remains."
**Response**: This treats Realms 2 and 3 as completely separate, which misunderstands the structure. Realm 3 (valence/normativity) is accessible *through* Realm 2 (consciousness), specifically through certain phenomenal states.
Not all conscious experiences provide access to Realm 3—only valence experiences do. A being with consciousness but without any capacity for pleasure or pain would indeed lack access to normative facts. But once you have valence, you have the epistemic bridge.
Think of it this way:
Realm 1 → Realm 2: Physical beings without consciousness can't access phenomenal facts
Realm 2 → Realm 3: Conscious beings without valence can't access normative facts
Realm 2 (with valence) → Realm 3: Direct acquaintance with valence states *is* access to normative facts
The phenomenal and normative aren't in completely separate magisteria. Valence is the bridge—it's both phenomenal (a type of conscious experience) and normative (these experiences have intrinsic mattering-ness).
### **Internalist Realism is "Hard to Make Sense Of" (Carlsmith)
**
**Objection**: "How could normative facts reshape motivation through rational understanding? It's hard to see what sort of epistemic access we could have to normative facts such that comprehensive contact with them necessarily reshapes our motivations via rational causation."
**Response**: This objection assumes the Humean picture where all facts are motivationally inert—beliefs are one thing, desires another, and never the twain shall meet. But this is question-begging against internalist realism.
Direct acquaintance with valence states provides both epistemic access AND motivational force—not because facts somehow magically move us, but because valence states are intrinsically motivationally significant. To know them through direct acquaintance just IS to be motivated by them (at least pro tanto).
Consider: when you experience intense suffering, you don't first learn a motivationally inert fact ("this is suffering") and then have a separate desire appear ("I want this to stop"). The awfulness and the motivation are intrinsic to the experience itself. This isn't mysterious—it's just how valence works.
The Humean wants to separate fact-grasping from motivation as a matter of metaphysical necessity. But this assumes all facts belong to Realm 1 (third-person observable properties). Realm 3 facts—normative facts accessible through valence—have a different structure. Their very nature involves mattering.
### **Arbitrariness and the Envelope Objection
**
**Objection**: "Moral realism seems arbitrary. The rules could be written in an envelope—there's no principled reason why *these* rules rather than *those* rules."
**Response**: This objection applies to traditional non-naturalist views where normative facts float above the natural world with no grounding. My view rejects this picture—normative facts are grounded in the phenomenology of valence (and possibly in other ways, accessible through higher epistemic realms).
The Asymmetry Test reveals the non-arbitrariness: Which scenario is more plausible?
Alien civilizations building bliss/experience machines
Alien civilizations building torture machines for their own sake
The first is far more plausible. This asymmetry isn't arbitrary—it reflects something real about the nature of valence. The idea that suffering is good and pleasure is bad is absurd, revealing genuine normative structure.
Additionally, consider the moral systems actually developed: hedonic utilitarianism, deontology, virtue ethics. These aren't random. They cluster around recognizing that suffering matters, that happiness matters, that persons matter. Very few moral systems seriously propose maximizing suffering for its own sake.
### **The Entanglement Objection
**
**Objection**: "For epistemology to work, natural creatures need to be 'entangled' with the domain they're learning about—in the right chain of touching and being touched. How can we be entangled with normative facts if they're non-natural?"
**Response**: We ARE entangled with normative facts through valence:
I experience blissful and suffering states
Through direct acquaintance, I know bliss is good and suffering is bad
This knowledge influences my actions
My actions affect the physical world
Normativity is infused into the natural world via consciousness. Through conscious beings' actions, normative facts have effects on the physical world. We're entangled with normative reality through phenomenal experience.
This is not mysterious. Consciousness bridges what people traditionally call the "physical world" and the normative world. The chain of touching and being touched exists—it just runs through phenomenal experience rather than through third-person observable causation alone.
### **Neither Aspect is Privileged
**
A final clarification about neutral monism: When I say physical and phenomenal are aspects of a single underlying substance with "neither aspect privileged," I mean that reductionism can run in either direction.
Materialists say: "Consciousness is just certain physical processes." Idealists say: "The physical is just certain structures in consciousness."
My view: Both moves conflate epistemic distinction with ontological reduction. The physical and phenomenal are epistemically distinct aspects of a deeper reality. We can describe the correspondence between them without privileging either aspect as ontologically fundamental.
This prevents the error of thinking normative facts must either be (a) reducible to Realm 1 properties, or (b) spookily floating above the physical. They're aspects of reality accessible through a specific epistemic mode—valence experience (and possibly other modes in higher realms).
## **Conclusion
**
Moral realism is true. Morality is not arbitrary. The good is necessarily good. But this becomes intelligible only when we:
Recognize that the view of reality is not divided into multiple epistemic realms
Embrace neutral monism to explain the intrinsic correspondence of phenomenal and physical
Accept that direct acquaintance with valence provides genuine normative knowledge
Recognize that our theoretical failures point to boundaries of our epistemic realm
Understand the Gödel-like encoding relationship between realms that makes the view both robustly realist and empirically testable
The wisest minds may discover deeper normative truths in higher epistemic realms. For us, something in the vicinity of hedonic considerations represents what we can access—not because we arbitrarily chose it, but because the phenomenology of valence reveals genuine normative facts. Whether hedonic utilitarianism specifically is correct, or something more nuanced, remains to be fully determined. But the non-arbitrariness of valence's normative significance is clear.
