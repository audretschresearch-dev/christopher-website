Luck
Jan 4 2025

[I’m aware of other conceptions of luck like “surface area of exposure to opportunities.” I’m talking about something else here]
Imagine you go to the casino and sit down at the blackjack table. You ask the dealer what the rules are. Is this a lucky thing to do? Do you seem like a lucky person?Certainly to the average casino-goer, you seem lucky. People love when this shit happens. “Beginner’s luck”, etc. Now, of course, the newbie at the blackjack table isn’t actually more likely to make money at blackjack. There’s no “real luck” here or whatever. But I wish to ask: why is asking the rules to blackjack in that scenario a lucky (seeming) thing to do?
I’ll just go straight to my answer. Being a first time blackjack player is lucky because it makes the “good” or “lucky” narratively satisfying. The idea here is that it’s all about narrative. If you act in such a way that winning is narratively satisfying / coherent, or if your story up until that point is one that would be better if you won than lost – if it actively *points *to you winning –... this is what I’m talking about. 
Imagine that you’re in a movie, or future you is telling the story of what you’re doing now: what can you do that makes a good ending more likely? Or: imagine that a base model LLM is predicting what’s going to happen.
If you want to be lucky, then your story should be better if you win. On the flipside, unlucky people live in such a way that their story is better if they lose. So part of understanding luck is understanding what makes a story “good” in the relevant sense. 

Of course in some sense luck is not a thing. Certainly this casino luck I’m talking about isn’t. Let’s move out of the casino domain. We might think of this as being “narrative-luck”. One claim is that trying to be lucky in this narrative sense is a good heuristic. The proof of this is left as an exercise for the reader. (It is true, but I’m writing on a time limit right now.)
Consider a kid who for one reason or another gets labeled “lucky”. Snce people believe that he’s lucky, they trust his calls, give him their money to invest, blah blah blah. It becomes a self fulfilling prophecy. So that’s a kind of mechanistic explanation, but also note how this is true in narrative as well: the person who is lucky as a kid and a teenager will also be lucky later and later.

A key idea here is the emphasis on *narrative.* You want your narrative to be one in which good things end up happening to you. If your narrative is currently on a bad track, or points in the wrong way? Ask yourself: what can I do that would change the implied endpoint of this narrative? 

I really struggle with creating personal narratives for myself to tell other people. That’s why I underperform interviews. I cringe and falter when asked “tell me your life story”. This is a weakness of mine. I’ve known it’s a weakness for a while and that I should just figure this shit out, but this luck thing really convinces me. By the way – I have no problem telling stories and constructing narratives! But I had some sort of block about doing it around myself. And I think that having this idea (and constraint!) that my narrative should be lucky is going to help me here.

Which brings us to our last point: when constructing your narrative (I’m tempted to write “when narrativizing reality” instead here), make sure it’s lucky. The specifics of how to do this? Ahem, further research needed.

I haven’t tried LLMs here yet, but I suspect they’ll be really helpful when asked about this stuff, because they understand narrative so well.
