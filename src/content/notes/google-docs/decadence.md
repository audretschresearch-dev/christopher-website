People need something “higher than themselves” to orient towards — something like church, religion, nation, tribe, or movement. In absence of “something higher”, people orient towards money/consumerism. The money/consumerism orientation is unfulfilling on a personal scale and leads to decadence (widespread moral+cultural decline) on a society scale.One can see this pattern play out in many places but it’s particularly important and clear in America. Church and communities began declining post WWII and consumerism became an orientation. This got especially bad in the 70s/80s. But still, there was the Idea of America. I.e. A sense of nation and America-as-an-ideal/vision, which in a 9 year period (2016-2025) basically disappeared. Thus we have current society.
There is declining quality, but it's not in consumer goods. It's in society, culture, and morality. The word is Decadence.
Homeless people, property theft, attitude from service workers (remember when this country was known for good service?)... nobody cares about anything higher than themselves.
Actually it's quite interesting that people mistakenly see quality declining in consumer goods when consumer goods are the one thing that have so clearly gotten better! The example in the article of car longevity is crazy to me, because cars have genuinely just gotten so much more durable, better, and higher quality in the past 30 years. (Remember American cars in the 70s-00s?). The article also quotes someone using food as an example. Food! Food quality has gotten **so** much better in the past 30 years!
Clothing is a more interesting example, as it probably has actually significantly decreased in durability, but how much of that is due to fast fashion vs new fabrics that have advantages over cotton but are less durable? My climbing pants are amazing but they won't last as long as my jeans, which maybe only last 3 years instead of 10 because they have some stretch fabric in them that makes them way more comfortable.
**It's almost as if everyone feels the very real "vibe" of declining quality, but they misattribute it to the consumer domain rather than to the social/cultural domain.
