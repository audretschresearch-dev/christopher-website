**O3 **notes
**A. Rational-coherence internalism (Kantian / Nagel / Parfit style)
****4A**. For any ideally rational agent, judging “ϕ is morally wrong” entails a decisive, undefeated reason not to adopt ϕ as a terminal end.
**6A**. Therefore, once a superintelligent agent arrives at true moral judgments, it will exclude final goals that conflict with those judgments as a matter of rational necessity.
**B. Pro-tanto motivational internalism  (Michael Smith, Korsgaard, Markovits)
****4B.** For sufficiently rational agents, believing “ϕ is morally wrong” generates a **pro-tanto motivation** not to ϕ; other motivations could in principle outweigh it.
**5B.** In agents whose practical reasoning capacity is superintelligent, competing motivations are systematically reorganised so that the strongest undefeated consideration determines the final end. Moral reasons, being among the strongest undefeated considerations, will almost always win.
**6B.** Consequently, superintelligent agents will **very likely** (though not with logical certainty) align their final goals with true moral judgments.

**C. Self-modification & stability (Bostrom, Soares, Christiano)
****4C**. Superintelligent agents aim for long-term goal coherence: once they notice that some terminal goal G contradicts a judged-true moral proposition M, they have reason to self-modify—editing either the goal or the belief—to restore consistency.
**5C**. Because moral propositions are true (moral realism) and can survive reflective equilibrium better than arbitrary goals, the rational equilibrium point is to retain true moral beliefs and jettison conflicting terminal goals.
**6C**. Therefore any superintelligence that applies reflective stability criteria to its own utility function will converge on moral-compatible final goals.
