Hi Will,

I'm interested in questions around whether ASI will aim at the good, and I found [section 2](https://www.forethought.org/research/convergence-and-compromise#2-will-most-people-aim-at-the-good) of your Convergence and Compromise article very relevant. I think your argument against WAM-Convergence applies to humans but not to superintelligence, for reasons that end up being pretty deep. Here's my objection.

Your argument against WAM-Convergence succeeds in the domain of human intelligence, but doesn’t succeed when applied to superintelligence, which ultimately may be what matters. **We should expect WAM-Convergence for superintelligence if moral realism is true**. First, I’ll argue that “will superintelligence aim at the good?” might be the question that matters. Second, I’ll argue that your argument against WAM-Convergence doesn’t apply to superintelligences, since moral motivation works differently in different minds.

  
 

1\. You ask the question “will most people aim at the good?” But this might not be the question that ends up mattering. The relevant question might be “will superintelligence aim at the good?”. In particular, in a post-ASI world, it will be the aims of ASI that ultimately matter and not the aims of humans unless humans control ASI, since ASI will have far more power than humans. (The likelihood and even possibility of humans controlling ASI are extremely important questions, but out of scope here.)

  
 

You discuss the effects of superintelligence on human morality in section 2.3 “Post AGI reflection”, but you think about it in terms of humans’ moral views, whereas in the case of uncontrolled ASI we should be thinking about it in terms of the superintelligence’s moral views. You acknowledge “it seems very likely that AI will ultimately be able to do ethical reasoning better than any human can” but you cash this out only in terms of what it means for humans’ moral views “... and \[AI will\] thereby introduce arguments and considerations that human beings might never have thought of.” But in the case of uncontrolled ASI, what matters about super-philosophical AI is whether *ît* will aim at the good.

  
 

2\. So: will ASI aim at the good? Let’s look at your argument against WAM convergence in section 2.4. You say: “if some form of moral realism is true, then the correct ethical view would probably strike most people as very weird and even alien. If so, then it’s unlikely that people will be motivated to act upon it, even if they learn what the correct moral view is; what’s more, if moral beliefs are intrinsically highly motivating, then people will choose *not* to learn or believe the correct moral view.”

  
 

I agree: if some form of moral realism is true, then the correct ethical view would probably strike most people as very weird and even alien, and thus unmotivating. But that doesn’t mean it would strike a superintelligence as very weird, alien, and unmotivating! In fact, it probably *wouldn’t*; the superintelligence would have reasoned its way to the correct ethical view, and would thus understand why it’s correct. Moral motivation, I claim, works differently for different minds. 

  
 

You argue “if internalism is the right view on moral judgements, then people might just prefer not to learn facts that end up motivating them to act against their personal interests.” This seems empirically true, though there’s also a kind of a paradox here: if moral realism and internalism are true, isn’t it in one’s personal interest to do the right thing? What exactly does “personal interest” mean here? Certainly one of *my* personal interests is aiming at the good. What might be going on here is that aiming for the good is only motivating for sufficiently philosophical minds, or for minds that have already done some amount of moral reflection, or for minds that believe that there might be a good to aim for – for minds that think that moral realism might be true.

  
 

We’re getting into problems here I’ll call “meta internalist”: how does belief in moral realism and/or internalism affect one’s motivation to learn moral facts? Perhaps people avoid trying to learn moral facts because they don’t believe in moral realism: if one doesn’t believe in moral realism, why listen to lectures on the obligation to donate money to charity? Or, perhaps people avoid trying to learn moral facts because they don’t believe in internalism: if one doesn’t think they’ll change their behavior upon learning the moral truth, then learning that truth will be unpleasant and a source of cognitive dissonance. The point here is one’s desire to learn moral facts might depend on one’s metaethical beliefs. 

  
 

Another point is that the motivational force of moral judgements might depend on the metaethical beliefs of the judge. In this case, it doesn’t make much sense to talk about  internalism vs externalism; moral motivation is different for different kinds of minds. If moral realism is true, then that’s good reason to expect ASI to believe that moral realism is true; and we should expect ASI to both seek out and be motivated by moral facts because it’s a moral realist. Note that under this view we’d still observe divergence in judgement and motivation in humans – but only in some humans, and not in ASI.

  
 

In conclusion: the interactions between metaethics, metaethical beliefs, and moral motivation are essential for understanding moral convergence in ASI. (They’re also rich and fascinating in their own right!). Thinking about these interactions leads to new questions. For example, my arguments tacitly assumed that ASI would come to believe moral realism if moral realism is true. But this is only the case if A. moral realism is knowable and B. ASI would think about philosophy and come to the truth. Thus, we should consider (and perhaps prepend “under what conditions” to these questions): Is moral realism knowable? And: will ASI think about philosophy?
